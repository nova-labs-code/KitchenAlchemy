Week 1 — Taste & Foundations

Navigate Days

Day 1 — The Four Forces of Flavor

Goal: Identify salt, fat, acid, and heat in foods.

Lesson:

  • Salt: Taste a plain boiled potato. Add salt gradually, noting changes. Try kosher, sea, and table salts.
  • Fat: Cook tomato or zucchini in butter vs olive oil vs plain. Observe texture, flavor, and mouthfeel.
  • Acid: Add lemon juice, vinegar, or yogurt to the same ingredient. Notice how it brightens flavors.
  • Heat: Roast vs steam vs sauté a vegetable. Observe flavor, color, and aroma changes.
Exercise: Taste 4 versions of one vegetable: plain, salted, fat-added, acid-added. Record notes in a flavor journal.
Mini Challenge: Try pairing two of the forces together (e.g., fat + acid) and note what flavor emerges.

Day 2 — Layering Seasoning

Goal: Build flavor like a professional chef.

  • Seasoning early creates a foundation; mid-cook adjustments refine the flavor; final seasoning sharpens the dish.
  • Compare adding all seasoning at once versus layering in stages.
  • Notice how timing changes depth and complexity.
Exercise: Make a vegetable stir-fry. Add salt early, adjust mid-cook, and finish at the end. Compare flavor depth.
Mini Challenge: Add a small pinch of acid at the end and see how it changes the dish’s brightness.
Journaling: Record what you noticed from timing seasoning at different stages.

Day 3 — Knife Skills & Uniform Cuts

Goal: Precision cutting for even cooking and presentation.

  • Practice julienne, dice, batonnet, brunoise on vegetables.
  • Uniform pieces cook evenly and enhance plate presentation.
  • Time yourself to improve efficiency without sacrificing accuracy.
Exercise: Chop three vegetables using two techniques each. Cook them and compare textures and doneness.
Mini Challenge: Make a visually appealing vegetable medley using only two types of cuts.

Day 4 — Heat Control

Goal: Learn how cooking methods affect flavor.

  • Sear, roast, steam, or sauté vegetables or proteins; note flavor and texture differences.
  • Understand which foods benefit from which cooking method.
  • Observe caramelization, Maillard reaction, and texture changes.
Exercise: Cook the same vegetable three ways: roast, steam, and sauté. Take detailed flavor and texture notes.
Mini Challenge: Combine two cooking methods in one dish (e.g., roast then sauté).

Day 5 — Mise en Place

Goal: Organize ingredients efficiently for professional workflow.

  • Pre-measure spices, chop vegetables, portion proteins.
  • Notice how preparation saves time and reduces mistakes.
  • Set up workstation for a single dish; clean as you go.
Exercise: Prepare a simple dish with full mise en place. Time yourself.
Mini Challenge: Cook the dish again, reducing preparation time while maintaining accuracy.

Day 6 — Tasting & Journaling

Goal: Train your palate and record observations systematically.

  • Taste multiple dishes made earlier in the week.
  • Note seasoning, flavor balance, texture, and aroma.
  • Reflect on what changes could improve each dish.
Exercise: Create a tasting board of 3–5 items from the week. Take detailed notes in a flavor journal.
Mini Challenge: Adjust seasoning on one dish based on your journal notes and taste the difference.

Day 7 — Weekly Challenge

Goal: Apply all lessons from the week in one composed dish.

  • Use seasoning, heat control, knife skills, and mise en place.
  • Focus on balance, texture, flavor, and presentation.
  • Document your workflow and reflect on strengths and areas to improve.
Exercise: Create a dish combining 2–3 vegetables and a protein. Apply all Week 1 lessons. Photograph/record results.
Mini Challenge: Present the dish as if for a Michelin-style tasting menu.
Journaling: Write 3–5 notes on what you learned this week and what to focus on in Week 2.
